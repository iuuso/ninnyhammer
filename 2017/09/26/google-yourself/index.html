Finding Out What the Web Knows About You

Ninnyhammer
2017-09-26
privacy, google

Users like me, who have used the Internet and the various different online services for well over a decade now, might have - or most definitely have - accounts on services that have been forgotten ages ago. Forums, early social media sites, movie rating sites, you name it.

You might have forgotten the sign-in credentials, and in the worst case scenario, also you might have either forgotten the email address or you don’t have the access to that email anymore.

There’s also a possibility that if you have used Google or Facebook or other login provider for signing on to a mobile game or something, that that platform has an public forum or profile that you have forgotten to opt out of.

One reason why this is important is that you could find information online about yourself that you don’t want to be available anymore or the information is outdated. The most obvious reason is that you wouldn’t want to have that kind of information there that has no proper purpose for being out there.

Arguably the most important phase in social engineering is the information gathering-phase. There’s two types of information available: “Traditional” and “non-traditional”. This post focuses on the traditional method.

The social-engineering.org defines the traditional method as follows:

“Traditional” sources are typically open, publicly available sources
of information that don’t require any illegal activity to obtain.

This post has suggestions on what kind of information you should be looking for and how you should be looking for them.

1. Google your name

The most obvious first step is to google your name and browse through the results

  • If your name has changed, look for the previous name as well.

If the results have sites or services that you are not using at the moment you should consider disabling the account. Or, if you want to get rid of it altogether, find out how you can delete the account. What’s the point of being registered to a service that you don’t have any use for?

2. Google your handle

If you have a specific handle that you use throughout different services (Instagram, Twitter, etc.) you also want to look for that as well.

Also, before they were called handles, the name you identified with most services was called a nickname. If you remember having different nicknames in use during some era of your past then make sure that you also look for that.

At this step you might want to check whether some of the social media accounts or others have links or information about who you are. For example, if you have a reddit account that you want to keep anonymous, view your profile as an outsider. What kind of information can be gathered from the posts that visible? By default, the posts are not hidden. That’s one of the reasons why people use a lot of different throw-away accounts when participating in threads and posting information that could be used to link the information to the people behind the accounts.

3. Use Different Search Engines

There are differences on how different search engines behave. For example, DuckDuckGo uses a very different search algorithm than Google and has a somewhat different approach for searching the internet. Google is a semantic search engine, when DuckDuckGo is a keyword search engine. Doing searches in different search engines most likely yields different results.

Also note: when searching for your name in Google, make sure that you do it in incognito mode (or private mode, what ever the term is in your preferred browser). This makes sure, that you won’t get the results that are tailored to your likings. Also, if you’re not using a VPN for hiding your IP address and securing your connection I highly suggest looking into that as well.

There are also search engines that are tailored specifically for searching people, and garnering information about individuals from different places. One of those places is pipl.com. It’s an interesting consept, be sure to check that out too.

Conclusion

These instructions are very rudimentary steps for managing your personal information online. Atleast you should be aware of what kind of information people could find about you with just basic search engine queries.

Times and situations change, so most likely the information you want to share with the public also changes.

Take the matter at your own hands and decide yourself who kind of information about you is available to the public. The services that you have been using propaply know much more stuff about you that you would be willing to bet on, but that’s a topic for another post.
